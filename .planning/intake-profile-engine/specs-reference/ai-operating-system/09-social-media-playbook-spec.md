# Document Production Spec 09: Social Media Playbook

## What This Spec Is

This is the production specification for Document 9 of the 12-document AI Operating System. It tells anyone building this document — Joshua, Claude in a fresh project, a subcontractor, or a future hire — exactly what goes in, what each section looks like, what to ask the client to fill it out, and how to know when it's done.

The Social Media Playbook defines how the business shows up on social platforms: which platforms, what content pillars, what cadence, what captions sound like, what hashtags matter, what gets handed to AI tools to draft and what stays human-touched. Without this document, social feeds drift in voice, post frequency swings from daily to nothing, hashtag strategy is ad-hoc, and AI-generated social content either feels generic or misses the platform-specific nuances that make content actually perform.

The job of this document is to produce a playbook that's operational, not aspirational. It tells the business exactly what to post, how often, in what voice, with what supporting assets — sized to the business's actual production capacity.

All captions adhere to the Brand Voice Guide. Additional social-specific rules:

**Hook (first 1-2 lines):** [Do this specific thing — state the thing, ask a direct question, share a specific fact]

**Context (2-4 sentences):** [Fill in the situation, background, or explanation]

**Takeaway / point (1-2 sentences):** [What the reader should leave with]

**CTA if applicable (1 sentence):** [Clear next action — only if it fits]

**Tags and hashtags:** [Separated from caption body by line breaks or in first comment per platform conventions]

### Platform-specific adjustments

**Instagram feed post:** 125-300 words, 3-5 paragraph breaks, up to 30 hashtags in first comment

**Instagram Story:** No caption needed; text overlay on image/video
**Facebook post:** 40-80 words, minimal hashtags (1-3)
**LinkedIn post:** 150-400 words, 2-5 hashtags, no emoji overload
**TikTok:** 100-150 character caption, 3-5 hashtags
**Pinterest:** Title is critical (keyword-rich), description 200 characters
**Twitter/X:** 280 character single post or threaded series

### Examples of strong captions

Include 3-5 real examples of captions that hit the brand voice well. Each labeled with pillar and platform. These become the reference set for AI generation and human editing.

### Examples of what to avoid

Include 3-5 examples of captions that drift off-brand or fall into social media traps. Each labeled with what's wrong.

### Section 7: AI-Assisted Content Generation

**Purpose:** How AI tools get used to draft social content, and what's off-limits.

**Word count target:** 300-500 words.

**Required structure:**

```markdown
## 7. AI-Assisted Content Generation

### What AI can draft

- **First drafts of captions for photo/video posts** — the human provides the asset and context; AI drafts; human edits
- **Hashtag set suggestions** — AI proposes; human reviews
- **Post copy variations for A/B testing** — AI produces 3-5 variants on a theme
- **Responses to simple DMs** — FAQ-aligned questions (see Document 07)
- **Caption translations** — if the business serves multi-lingual audiences

### What AI MUST NOT do without human review

- **Publish without human approval** in the first 90 days of any AI workflow
- **Generate captions for sensitive content** (testimonials, customer stories, anything involving real named customers)
- **Make factual claims** not grounded in the Business Facts Reference
- **Respond to complaint or negative comment DMs**
- **Generate posts featuring identifiable minors** (critical for preschool and family-category businesses)

### AI prompt structure

Every AI social-caption generation prompt includes:

1. **Brand Voice Guide context** (either full doc or voice attributes section pasted in)
2. **Pillar** the post belongs to
3. **Asset description** (what the photo/video shows)
4. **Specific details** (who, what, when, where, why)
5. **CTA goal** (or explicit "no CTA needed")
6. **Platform and length target**
7. **Banned phrases reminder**
8. **Request for 3 variants** so the human has options to edit from

### Example AI prompt

```
You are generating an Instagram caption for [Business Name].

Brand voice: [Paste voice attributes from Brand Voice Guide].

Pillar: [Which of the 4-6 content pillars this post belongs to].

Asset: [Description of the photo/video — e.g., "Wide shot of the ceremony lawn at golden hour, empty, late September light, no people in frame"].

Specific context: [E.g., "This was taken during a real wedding last month. The couple got married at 4:45pm. The light in October hits this lawn in a specific way that's hard to replicate."]

Length target: 150-250 words.

CTA: None needed — this is an atmosphere post.

Banned phrases: [Paste list from Brand Voice Guide].

Generate 3 caption options that apply the voice attributes. Each should feel like a different angle on the same asset.
```

### Quality control

- Human review every AI-drafted caption before publishing
- Monthly audit: do AI-drafted posts perform similarly to human-drafted posts? If not, prompt adjustments.
- Quarterly: review the Brand Voice Guide doc for drift based on what AI has been producing.
```

### Section 8: DM and Engagement Response Protocols

**Purpose:** How the business handles incoming DMs, comments, mentions, and tags.

**Word count target:** 200-400 words.

**Required structure:**

```markdown
## 8. DM and Engagement Response Protocols

### DM response SLA

- **Business hours (M-F 9-5):** [Time target — e.g., "Within 4 hours"]
- **Evenings and weekends:** [Time target — usually "Next business morning"]
- **Who owns DM response:** [Person]

### DM categorization

Every incoming DM falls into one of these categories:

**Category 1: Sales inquiry**
- **Action:** Respond within SLA. If substantive, move to email immediately: "Great to hear from you — my booking coordinator handles all inquiries. Want me to have her reach out, or she can be reached at [email]."
- **Never:** Conduct full sales conversations in DM. Move to email.

**Category 2: FAQ question**
- **Action:** Answer using FAQ Bank (Document 07) language. Keep brief. Offer follow-up via email if more depth needed.

**Category 3: Existing customer communication**
- **Action:** Respond warmly. Route to booking coordinator if it's about logistics or planning.

**Category 4: Vendor or partner outreach**
- **Action:** Route to appropriate person; don't handle yourself unless you are that person.

**Category 5: Spam / solicitation / low-quality outreach**
- **Action:** Ignore or polite brush-off. Don't engage in back-and-forth.

**Category 6: Negative / complaint**
- **Action:** Acknowledge within SLA; move to email or phone for resolution. Never argue in DM. Follow Document 07's negative-review response principles.

### Comment response protocols

- **Positive comments:** Like + brief reply within 24 hours
- **Questions in comments:** Respond publicly with brief answer + "DM us for more details" if substantive
- **Negative comments:** Do not argue publicly. Respond briefly and professionally. Offer to DM to resolve.
- **Tagged photos / mentions:** Repost to Stories with credit when appropriate; thank in DM

### Mentions and UGC handling

- **Customer posts tagging us:** Repost to Story with credit (where platform allows). Save image to content library. Add to testimonial/UGC backlog.
- **Vendor posts tagging us:** Like, comment briefly, consider reposting depending on context.
- **Press/media tags:** Screenshot, save, route to the press log in Document 04.

### Engagement initiation (outbound)

- **Follow pattern:** Follow relevant vendors, past customers (with permission), industry voices. Don't follow personal accounts of customers unless invited.
- **Commenting on others' posts:** Natural engagement on vendor/partner content. Don't spam-comment for visibility.
- **DM outreach:** Only cold outreach to known warm contacts (planner referrals, vendor introductions). No cold sales DMs.
```

### Section 9: Measurement and Optimization

**Purpose:** What success looks like and how to improve over time.

**Word count target:** 200-400 words.

**Required structure:**

```markdown
## 9. Measurement and Optimization

### Monthly tracking metrics

| Metric | Current baseline | 90-day target | 12-month target |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary platform followers | [#] | [#] | [#] |
| Primary platform engagement rate | [%] | [%] | [%] |
| DM inquiries per month | [#] | [#] | [#] |
| Website clicks from social | [#] | [#] | [#] |
| Story view completion rate | [%] | [%] | [%] |

### What's NOT the right metric

Follower count alone is often misleading. Specific metrics that deserve less attention:

- Raw follower count without engagement context
- Like count on a viral post that doesn't convert
- Impressions without click-through

### What IS the right metric

Depends on the platform's role (per Section 2):

- If the platform is for **awareness**: impressions + reach + new followers from non-branded discovery
- If the platform is for **consideration**: profile visits, website clicks, saves
- If the platform is for **conversion**: DM inquiries, link clicks to inquiry form, booked appointments sourced from platform

### Quarterly optimization review

Every quarter, review:

1. Which posts performed top 20% — what do they have in common?
2. Which posts performed bottom 20% — what do they have in common?
3. Which pillars are over-producing (high content, low engagement)?
4. Which pillars are under-producing (few posts, high engagement when posted)?
5. Are any platforms trending down hard enough to demote or abandon?
6. Are any platforms emerging where the business should test a presence?

### Annual playbook refresh

Full playbook review every 12 months or when any of these change:
- Platform algorithm or feature shifts meaningfully
- Business offering changes (new service, new customer segment)
- Brand voice evolves
- Production capacity changes meaningfully
```

## How to Build This Document

The full process, in order. Total time: 3-4 hours.

### Step 1: Pre-Build Prep (15 minutes, Joshua)

Confirm prerequisite documents are delivered. Request access to social accounts if not already granted. Pull the Content Library (Document 04) for asset inventory context.

### Step 2: Platform Audit (45 minutes, Joshua)

For each active platform:
- Scroll the last 30-50 posts
- Note top-performing and bottom-performing posts
- Document follower count, engagement rate, posting frequency
- Note voice consistency
- Note response pattern to DMs

### Step 3: Competitor Social Scan (30 minutes, Joshua)

From Document 05 competitor list, check each competitor's social:
- Their posting frequency
- Their content mix
- Their hashtag patterns
- Their engagement levels
- Anything they do well that informs the client's strategy

### Step 4: Pillar Identification (30 minutes, Joshua)

Based on:
- What the business is known for (from Brand Voice Guide)
- What performs in content history (Document 04 Section 7)
- What's in the Content Library to draw from (Document 04)
- Where the business wants to grow visibility (Document 05 positioning recommendations)

Identify 4-6 content pillars.

### Step 5: Draft Generation (45-60 minutes, Claude)

In the consulting Claude Project, run this prompt:

```
Generate a Social Media Playbook for [Business Name] using Document Production Spec 09 as the structure. Industry: [industry].

Source materials:

[Paste: Brand Voice Guide — voice attributes and banned phrases]

[Paste: Business Facts Reference — relevant facts that posts might reference]

[Paste: Content Library — asset inventory, themes, and gaps]

[Paste: Competitor Landscape Brief — social observations]

[Paste: SEO Strategy — topic alignment]

[Paste: Platform audit notes]

[Paste: Pillar identification notes — the 4-6 content pillars]

[Paste: Client's stated production capacity from Document 04 Section 8]

Build all 9 sections following the spec. Critical: Section 6 (Cadence) must be sized to the client's actual production capacity. Don't recommend 5 posts per week if production capacity is 2 per week.

For Section 3 (Content Pillars), every pillar should include 3-5 example post descriptions that could realistically be posted in the next 30 days using existing Content Library assets.

For Section 7 (AI-Assisted Content Generation), include the specific prompt template the client will use when generating captions.
```

### Step 6: Joshua Review (30 minutes)

For each section, check:
- Section 2: Are platform priorities honest about where the business's audience is?
- Section 3: Are pillars specific and distinct? Test: could a post fit into two pillars? If yes, pillars overlap too much.
- Section 4: Do caption examples pass the brand voice test?
- Section 5: Are hashtag sets specific and not generic (#love, #wedding without context)?
- Section 6: Is the cadence realistic given Document 04 capacity?
- Section 7: Is the AI prompt template complete and usable?
- Section 8: Are response protocols specific enough to action?
- Section 9: Are metrics tied to platform role, not generic vanity metrics?

### Step 7: Client Review (30 minutes client time)

Send to client with this email:

```
Subject: Social Media Playbook — sustainable plan, not aspirational

Attached: the Social Media Playbook for [Business Name].

Two things I want you to pay attention to:

1. Section 2 (Platform Priorities). Confirm my reading that [primary platform] is where we invest, and the rest are maintained-not-invested. If you disagree, tell me now — changes the rest of the playbook.

2. Section 6 (Cadence). This is sized to produce [X posts per week]. Realistic for you? Too much? Too little? If it's wrong, we adjust now. Better to hit a lower cadence consistently than miss a higher one.

3. Section 3 (Content Pillars). Do these feel right, or is there a pillar I'm missing / one that's not really a pillar for you?

The rest is reference — read at whatever depth you want.
```

### Step 8: Final Edits and Delivery (15 minutes)

- Apply client edits
- Save with naming convention `[client-slug]-09-social-media-playbook-v1.0.md`
- Upload to client's Claude Project as knowledge

## Common Failure Modes

**Failure 1: Cadence is aspirational, not realistic.** Playbook recommends 5 Instagram posts and 3 Reels per week for a business producing 1 piece per week. Predictable failure. Fix: Section 6 must reference Document 04 Section 8 capacity directly.

**Failure 2: Pillars are too abstract.** "Inspiration" and "Community" aren't pillars — they're vibes. A pillar should name specific content. Fix: every pillar must have 3-5 concrete example posts to pass definition-of-done.

**Failure 3: All platforms are Tier 1.** The business tries to invest equally in 4 platforms and fails on all of them. Fix: force ranking in Section 2. No ties allowed.

**Failure 4: AI-generation section is permissive.** "Use AI for captions" without prompt structure or must-not-do list leads to off-brand content. Fix: Section 7 must include specific prompt template and hard limits.

**Failure 5: DM protocols get ignored.** Section 8 is read but not operationalized. DMs keep getting handled inconsistently. Fix: Section 8 should feed directly into whoever owns DMs and ideally into a written checklist they reference.

**Failure 6: Measurement drifts to vanity.** Team starts tracking follower count alone and congratulates itself while inquiries flatline. Fix: Section 9 must explicitly name "not the right metric" items and defend what IS the right metric by platform role.

**Failure 7: Competitor copying vs. differentiation.** Playbook inadvertently replicates competitor patterns instead of differentiating. Fix: voice differentiation analysis from Document 05 Section 5 must be integrated into Section 4.

# Audrey's Farmhouse Social Media Playbook

**Prepared by:** Joshua Brown / Upriver Consulting
**Date:** April 2026
**Version:** 1.0

**Companion documents:** Documents 01, 02, 04, 05, 06.

---

## 1. Current State Audit

### Instagram: @audreysfarmhouse
- Followers: 14,800
- Total posts: 847 (since 2019)
- Posting frequency (last 90 days): ~3 posts per week (inconsistent — ranges 1-5)
- Engagement rate: 3.4% (strong for this size account)
- Top-performing recent posts: October real wedding carousel (1,240 likes), Chef behind-the-scenes reel (28K views), Property golden-hour reel (22K views)
- Bottom-performing: Generic "weekend vibes" posts, testimonial quotes on graphics (both under 300 likes)
- Content mix currently: 60% photo, 25% reel, 10% carousel, 5% story
- Voice consistency: Strong when booking coordinator writes; drifts generic when batch-scheduled without caption attention

## 3. Content Pillars

- **Asset types:** Photo carousels (8-10 images), Reels (30-60 sec), single hero photos

- **Asset types:** Wide-shot property photos, drone footage, golden-hour reels, seasonal scenes

## 4. Caption Voice and Structure

### Caption voice rules

All captions apply the Brand Voice Guide. Additionally:

- **Length:** 125-300 words for feed posts; 50-150 for Reels
- **Opening line:** First sentence states something specific. No "Just had to share this..." or "Check this out..."
- **Paragraph breaks:** Yes, every 2-4 sentences
- **Voice patterns:** First-person singular when the coordinator or owner is narrating ("I love this shot because..."). Never "we love" or "our special day."
- **Emoji policy:** Minimal. Occasional single-emoji accent at end of a section is fine. Never emoji-heavy captions.
- **CTA norm:** Most posts don't need a CTA. Real wedding posts can end with "See more photos in our gallery — link in bio." Educational posts can end with "Full post on the blog — link in bio." Never "DM us for info!" (generic, salesy).
- **Questions in captions:** Only when genuinely asking. Never as engagement bait.

### Caption structure template

**Hook (first 1-2 lines):** A specific detail, observation, or statement. Not "We had the most magical..." Good hook: "The ceremony lawn has a particular light between 4:30 and 5:00 in mid-October."

**Context (2-4 sentences):** What the reader is seeing. Details about the wedding, the moment, the choice.

**Takeaway (1-2 sentences):** What makes this worth paying attention to. Often the differentiator or the insight.

**CTA if applicable (1 sentence):** Clear next action, if any.

**Tags and hashtags:** In first comment for Instagram.

### Platform-specific adjustments

**Instagram feed:** 125-300 words; paragraph breaks; hashtags in first comment
**Instagram Reel:** 50-150 words; 3-5 hashtags in caption; hook in first 3 seconds of video matters more than caption
**Instagram Story:** No caption; text overlay on image/video
**Pinterest:** Title rich with target keyword (from Document 06); 200-char description; keyword-rich

### Examples of strong captions

**Example 1 (Pillar: Real weddings):**

> The ceremony lawn in October does something specific with the 4:45 light. Anna and Devin got married here last month. Their photographer mentioned the light on their tour. They saw it for themselves on the day.
>
> What I love about this shot: they're both looking at each other. Not at the camera. Not at the crowd. Not checking their vows. Just at each other.
>
> That's the whole thing, isn't it.
>
> Anna + Devin | October 2024
> Photography: Joshua Brown Photography (@joshuabrownphotography)

**Example 2 (Pillar: Property and atmosphere):**

> Early morning before a Saturday wedding. The property before anyone arrives. Quiet enough to hear the river.
>
> It stays this way for about an hour.

**Example 3 (Pillar: Kitchen and food):**

> Saturday prep starts at 10am. Chef does the menu walk with the service team, checks every dish, and tastes everything before plating.
>
> The thing about in-house catering is it means one kitchen, one chef, one standard. No coordination between a catering team and a venue team. Everything comes from the same hands.

**Example 4 (Pillar: Educational):**

> Questions to ask any Hudson Valley wedding venue before you book:
>
> 1. What's included vs. what's separate?
> 2. Who runs the day?
> 3. What happens if it rains?
> 4. What's your 11pm plan?
> 5. Where are guests staying?
>
> Every venue will have different answers. The answers tell you more than the tour does.
>
> Full post in the blog — link in bio.

### Examples of what to avoid

- "We had the most magical October wedding last weekend!" (banned phrase, generic)
- "Tag your future wedding venue 🙌💍✨" (engagement bait, emoji overload)
- "Dreams do come true at Audrey's 🥰" (banned phrase, saccharine)
- "Obsessed with this couple 😍😍😍" (generic, emoji spam)
- "Our team goes above and beyond for every wedding" (hollow claim, banned phrase territory)

## 7. AI-Assisted Content Generation

### What AI can draft

- Publish anything without the coordinator's review in the first 90 days
- Generate captions for real wedding posts featuring specific couples without the coordinator pulling direct details
- Make factual claims not grounded in the Business Facts Reference
- Respond to any DM containing complaint, special circumstance, or emotional content
- Generate posts that include minors (N/A for weddings typically, but policy noted)

### AI prompt template

You are generating an Instagram caption for Audrey's Farmhouse, a boutique Hudson Valley wedding venue in Wallkill, NY.

Brand voice guide excerpt:

- Direct over diplomatic
- Warm but not effusive

## 8. DM and Engagement Response Protocols

### DM response SLA

- **Business hours (M-F 9-5):** Within 4 hours
- **Evenings and weekends:** Next business morning
- **Who owns DM response:** Booking coordinator (primary), Joshua as backup

### DM categorization

**Category 1: Sales inquiry (couple asking about dates, pricing, availability)**
- Action: Respond within SLA. Move to email immediately.
- Template: "Happy to share details. Easiest for me is to send everything through email — pricing, availability, tour options. What's the best email? And if there's a specific date you're considering, even better."

**Category 2: FAQ question (specific answerable question)**
- Action: Answer from FAQ Bank. Brief. Offer to follow up via email if depth needed.
- Template varies by question; use FAQ Bank (Document 07) language

**Category 3: Existing customer communication (already-booked couple)**
- Action: Respond warmly. Route to coordinator if about logistics or planning. Ask if they want to continue in DM or email.

**Category 4: Vendor or partner outreach**
- Action: Route to Joshua or coordinator as appropriate. Photographer inquiries go to Joshua; floral/DJ/planner inquiries go to coordinator.

**Category 5: Spam / solicitation / low-quality outreach**
- Action: Ignore or brief brush-off. No engagement.

**Category 6: Negative / complaint**
- Action: Acknowledge within SLA. Immediately move to email or phone. Never argue in DM.
- Template: "Thanks for reaching out. This is important and I want to make sure we get it right — can I get your email or a phone number so we can handle this properly?"

### Comment response protocols

- **Positive comments:** Heart + brief reply within 24 hours (e.g., "Thanks!" or specific acknowledgment)
- **Questions in comments:** Respond publicly with brief answer + "DM us for more" if substantive
- **Negative comments:** Do not argue publicly. Professional response only. Offer DM resolution.
- **Tagged photos / mentions:** Repost to Stories with credit when appropriate. Thank in DM.

### Mentions and UGC handling

- **Customer posts tagging us:** Repost to Story with credit (once per customer per event to avoid spam-reposting). Save image to content library.
- **Vendor posts tagging us:** Like and brief comment. Consider reposting if the content aligns with a pillar and is high quality.
- **Press/media tags:** Screenshot for press log (Document 04). Notify Joshua.

### Engagement initiation (outbound)

- **Follow pattern:** Follow relevant vendors, industry peers, past customers (who've opted in via tagging or permission), Hudson Valley publications
- **Commenting on others' posts:** Natural engagement on vendors we work with regularly. Don't spam-comment.
- **Cold DM outreach:** Not a standard practice. Exception: planner relationships that start at vendor introductions or in-person meetings move to DM organically.
