# Running the 90-minute Upriver client interview

The interview is the single highest-leverage hour of the engagement. Everything downstream — voice guide, design brief, fixes scope, copy direction — is only as good as what you extract here. A bad interview produces a generic rebuild.

## When to apply this

- Preparing to facilitate a client kickoff call.
- Reviewing the interview guide generated by `upriver interview-prep <slug>`.
- Post-interview: processing the transcript with `upriver process-interview`.
- Troubleshooting a thin or unusable transcript (go back and run a 30-minute follow-up).

## Pre-interview (T-24 hours)

1. Finish `upriver audit <slug>` and `upriver synthesize <slug>`. The interview is calibrated against findings — without them you're asking blind questions.
2. Read `clients/<slug>/interview-prep.md` end-to-end. Do not improvise — the prep doc has the canonical question order.
3. Skim `clients/<slug>/audit-package.json` → `findings` array. Flag the top 5 P0s — you'll reference them by plain-English impact during the call, never by finding ID.
4. Confirm a transcript capture method. Options in order of preference:
   - Dedicated transcription tool (Otter, Rev, Fathom).
   - Zoom / Meet cloud recording with auto-transcript.
   - Manual notes (last resort — you will miss 60% of the signal).
5. Send the client a one-paragraph pre-read: "We'll spend 90 minutes on who you serve, what's working, and what you want the new site to do. No tech talk — bring coffee."

## The 90-minute agenda

This is a loose schedule, not a rigid one. The order matters more than the exact times.

### Minutes 0-10 — Open loose, get them talking

Start with a question that doesn't have a right answer and that they've told the story of before:

- "Walk me through how you ended up doing this — I want the long version."

Do not interrupt. Let them ramble for 7-10 minutes. This surfaces the raw language that feeds the voice guide. If you talk in these 10 minutes, you've missed the point.

### Minutes 10-30 — Audience (the most important segment)

The goal here is to extract a specific, named customer. "Couples planning a wedding" is worthless. "Anna and Mike, 28, from Brooklyn, engaged in October, looking for a venue under 2 hours from the city that doesn't feel corporate" is gold.

Questions in priority order:

1. "Tell me about your best customer in the last year. Not your most profitable — the one where everything just _worked_. Who were they?"
2. "What's the thing they said or did that let you know they got it?"
3. "Now the opposite — the customer that was wrong for you. What made them wrong?"
4. "What do your best customers say to their friends about you? What's the referral sentence?"
5. "What do they Google to find someone like you? (If they don't know, have them guess.)"

Capture verbatim quotes. Do not paraphrase in real time.

### Minutes 30-50 — What they sell

Not the sales pitch. The _actual_ thing:

1. "If a customer could only take away one thing from the new site, what would it be?"
2. "What's the part of your offer that customers always get wrong, or don't understand until they've signed?"
3. "What's the price objection you hear most, and how do you handle it?"
4. "What's the one thing a competitor does well that you'd steal if you could?"
5. "What do you wish customers already knew about you before their first call?"

This segment is where the voice guide's `sampleBodyCopy` comes from. Quote aggressively.

### Minutes 50-70 — The current site (audit translation)

Only now do you introduce audit findings, and only the top 3-4 P0s. Translate each into plain English and ask them about it, not about the finding:

- Not: "Your site is missing canonical tags on 14 pages."
- But: "Google is treating duplicate versions of your venue page as separate pages — which means none of them rank as well as they could. When someone searches 'farmhouse wedding Catskills' right now, your competitors are showing up first. Did you know that?"

Their reaction to this tells you what to emphasize in the rebuild's messaging.

Also surface:
1. "What pages on your current site do you send links to the most?" (= these are the money pages)
2. "When someone calls or emails you, do they reference something they saw on the site? What do they reference?"
3. "What's the thing on your current site you've been meaning to fix for a year?"

### Minutes 70-85 — Constraints and assets

Practical:

1. "What photos do you have? Can you send them to us by Friday?"
2. "Who writes copy today? Are they available for a review pass?"
3. "What hard deadlines are on your calendar — a peak season, an event, a marketing push?"
4. "Who gets final say on the site — you, a partner, a marketing lead?"
5. "Anything we absolutely can't change — a tagline, a color, a URL structure?"

### Minutes 85-90 — Close

1. "What's the thing you're most nervous about in this rebuild?"
2. "If I told you we'd only hit 80% of what you want, what's the 20% you'd want us to hit?"
3. Confirm next steps, deliverable dates, and who reviews what.

## Facilitation tactics

- **Silence is a tool.** If they stop after 10 seconds, wait 8 more. 70% of the time they start again with something better than the first pass.
- **Never ask "do you agree with this finding?"** They'll say yes. Ask "does this match what you see in your inbox?" — forces them to check against their own data.
- **Quote back.** Every 15-20 minutes, paraphrase what you've heard: "So what I'm hearing is that your real differentiator is Sunday morning breakfast — not the ceremony space. Is that right?" Misunderstandings surface here or not at all.
- **Talkative clients:** redirect with "that's exactly what I needed — let me hold you there so we can get to the other questions." Do not let them keep going on a tangent beyond 5 minutes.
- **Quiet clients:** ask for specifics. Not "who's your audience?" but "tell me about the last person who booked you — walk me through the first email they sent."
- **Don't sell.** The interview is not a pitch. You've already been hired. Curiosity, not performance.

## Post-interview

1. Get the transcript within 24 hours while memory is fresh.
2. Run `upriver process-interview <slug> --transcript ./transcript.txt`. This updates `brand-voice-guide.md` and flags asset asks.
3. Read the updated voice guide. If it still sounds generic, the interview didn't go deep enough — schedule a 30-minute follow-up focused on the weakest section.
4. Save the raw transcript at `clients/<slug>/docs/interview-transcript.md`. Never delete it; it's the reference artifact for every copy decision downstream.

## What a good interview produces

When you're done, you should be able to answer these without guessing:

- What's the audience's exact phrasing for their problem?
- What makes this client different from the three closest competitors, in their own words?
- What's the one thing the new site must communicate, and what should it de-emphasize?
- What assets will the client deliver, and by when?

If you can't answer any of these, the interview was incomplete.
