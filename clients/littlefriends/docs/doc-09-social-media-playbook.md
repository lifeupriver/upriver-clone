## 2. Platform Priorities and Roles

### A note on what role social plays for this school

Before ranking platforms, it helps to be clear about what social media is actually for at Little Friends Learning Loft. The school operates at roughly 90% of licensed capacity and runs primarily on JCC word-of-mouth. Social is not a volume lead-generation channel. Its roles are more specific:

1. **Trust reinforcement for prospective families** already on a shortlist after a recommendation or a Google search. A family that finds the Instagram or Facebook Page and sees warm, specific classroom content moves closer to scheduling a tour.
2. **Community continuity for enrolled families.** Parents want glimpses of their children's classroom world. A consistent social presence that reflects actual classroom life, not stock photos or generic graphics, keeps the community warm between Brightwheel messages and newsletters.
3. **Discovery for families outside the JCC orbit.** A smaller but growing portion of inquiries comes from Google search (Document 05). Consistent social content with appropriate local hashtags supports that discovery path without requiring paid advertising.

### Tier 1: Facebook

**Role:** Primary public channel. The parent demographic (roughly ages 28-42, navigating a young child's first or next school placement) still concentrates on Facebook in this geographic market. The JCC community likely has existing Facebook presence that overlaps with this school's audience. Local parent groups in Newburgh and Orange County are also active on Facebook and are a secondary discovery path (Document 05).

- **Audience:** Parents of children ages 2-5, Newburgh and surrounding Orange County towns, with JCC community overlap
- **Cadence target:** 3 posts per week [NEEDS CONFIRMATION: pending workflow audit and capacity confirmation with Rebecca; may realistically start at 2 per week and build]
- **Content mix target:** Classroom-life posts, teacher and community voices, educational content, event announcements
- **Response SLA:** Same business day for DMs and comments (see Section 8)
- **Tracking metric:** Post reach from non-followers, profile visits, DM inquiries sourced to Facebook

### Tier 2: Instagram

**Role:** Visual portfolio and trust reinforcement. Instagram is where a prospective family browses to get a sense of the school's atmosphere and character before scheduling a tour. It reinforces the warm-small-not-a-factory positioning (Document 01). Secondary to Facebook for this audience but worth maintaining with consistent, well-chosen content that mirrors the strongest Facebook posts.

- **Audience:** Same parent demographic, skewing slightly younger; also reaches families who discover the school via Google Maps or a link from the JCC website
- **Cadence target:** 2-3 posts per week, drawing from the same content pool as Facebook
- **Content mix target:** 70% photo, 20% carousel, 10% Stories (reactive, not scheduled)
- **Response SLA:** Within 1 business day (see Section 8)
- **Tracking metric:** Profile visits, bio link clicks to website, post saves (a strong consideration signal), DM inquiries sourced to Instagram

### Tier 3: No active third platform at this time

The school does not have the production capacity to invest meaningfully in a third public platform. The right move is to do Facebook and Instagram well before adding anything else.

### Platforms explicitly skipped

- **TikTok:** Firm skip on child-privacy grounds. See Section 1.
- **Pinterest:** Low audience relevance for local preschool discovery in Newburgh.
- **YouTube:** Revisit when welcome video production begins.
- **LinkedIn, Twitter/X:** Not the audience.

### Why this priority order

Facebook comes first because the parent demographic and the JCC community are already there. The discovery path Document 05 describes (word of mouth through the JCC, then Google search, then local parent groups) maps most naturally to Facebook as a public platform. Instagram comes second because it is the right format for the visual content that defines the school's character, and because prospective families who do deeper research will check it. The school's social job is trust reinforcement at relatively small scale, not mass reach, which makes two platforms done well more valuable than five platforms done inconsistently.

## 3. Content Pillars

The account posts about five things. Every post falls into one of these pillars.

### Pillar 1: Classroom life, "the work"

- **What it covers:** Children engaged in Montessori work. Practical life activities (pouring, spooning, folding), sensorial materials, early numeracy work with the bead materials, grace and courtesy moments, the work cycle in progress. These are the warm classroom moments that teachers already capture and post to Slack `#photos`.
- **Why it matters:** This is the content that turns a browser into a tour request. A parent who sees a two-year-old independently at the water station, or a four-year-old concentrating on the bead chain, understands something about the school that no caption can carry alone.
- **Asset types:** Photo (primary); occasional photo carousel for a sequence (a practical life activity step-by-step, a before/after of a work cycle)
- **Caption tone:** Specific and quiet. Name the material or the activity. Let the photo carry the emotional weight.
- **Frequency target:** 2 of every 5 posts
- **Example post concepts:**
  - A child at the spooning tray, fully focused. Caption names the activity and briefly describes what control of error means in practice.
  - Two children at the bead cabinet, one showing the other. Caption notes that the mixed-age classroom produces this without teacher direction.
  - Practical life station in use. Caption: one sentence on what children are actually learning here (fine motor control, sequencing, independence) without using any of those words.
- **Consent gate:** Every photo in this pillar features identifiable children. Before any photo is scheduled, the child's consent status must be confirmed against the current reconciled spreadsheet. This step is mandatory. See Section 6 for where it sits in the posting workflow.

### Pillar 2: The prepared environment

- **What it covers:** The classroom, Montessori materials on shelves, the environment before children arrive or after the day ends. No identifiable children in frame.
- **Why it matters:** These photos can be used freely without consent tracking. They show the intentionality and quality of the prepared environment to prospective families. They also answer the common parent question: what does my child's classroom actually look like?
- **Asset types:** Photo (primary). Important caveat: Document 04 notes that dedicated environment-only photos do not yet exist. A half-day photo session before the school year begins, or during a scheduled break, would produce 30-40 reusable assets that can be drawn on for months. This is the single highest-ROI photo investment the school can make for social purposes.
- **Caption tone:** Calm, specific, Montessori-vocabulary comfortable. Describe what the parent is seeing. If using a Montessori term, define it in the next sentence.
- **Frequency target:** 1 of every 5 posts
- **Example post concepts:**
  - A shelf of sensorial materials, labels visible. Caption describes what one specific material does and how a child uses it.
  - The practical life station, set and ready before the morning begins. Caption explains what "prepared environment" actually means without just using the phrase.
  - The reading corner, books facing out (covers visible). Caption: how book selection works in a Montessori classroom and why books are displayed this way.
- **Consent gate:** None for environment-only photos. This pillar is the consent-safe workhorse when Pillar 1 content is moving through the approval queue.

### Pillar 3: Teacher and community voices

- **What it covers:** Miss Tova, Miss Carla, Miss Dana, and Rebecca. Their faces, brief first-person quotes, moments in the classroom. The people behind the school.
- **Why it matters:** Families research who will be with their child every day. A face and a name on social reduces the unknown before the tour. The school's character comes through the teachers as much as the materials.
- **Asset types:** Teacher photos (with or without children; children trigger the consent gate); text-image posts with a quote; brief career or philosophy carousels
- **Caption tone:** First-person where possible ("Miss Tova has been in the Twos classroom since..."). Specific about what this teacher brings.
- **Frequency target:** 1 of every 5 posts
- **Example post concepts:**
  - Rebecca at the enrollment table during open house. Caption covers her role, her direct contact, and ends with "come and see."
  - Miss Carla explaining the three-period lesson during a parent observation visit. Caption explains what the three-period lesson is.
  - A teacher quote from the week. One specific thing that happened or was noticed, in the teacher's own words.
- **Consent gate:** If children appear in the photo, consent check required. Teacher portraits without children present are freely usable.

### Pillar 4: Montessori explained

- **What it covers:** Short, specific explanations of Montessori concepts for parents who are curious but new to the method. Maps directly to the SEO long-tail clusters in Document 06 (the "Montessori method explained" cluster and the "Montessori philosophy questions" cluster that serves AI-search behavior).
- **Why it matters:** A prospective family doing research on Montessori will find this content and connect it to the school before they schedule a tour. It also serves families who have enrolled but want to understand the method better.
- **Asset types:** Text-forward photo posts with longer captions; 2-5 slide carousels with one concept per slide; single environment photo with an educational caption
- **Caption tone:** Direct, plain, zero condescension. Lead with the real description first, then the term (or skip the term entirely). These posts earn trust by being genuinely useful, not by performing authority.
- **Frequency target:** 1 of every 5 posts
- **Example post concepts:**
  - "What is the work cycle?" A carousel explaining the uninterrupted morning block, what it looks like, why it exists, and what a child does during it.
  - "Mixed-age classrooms: the honest explanation." Why children ages 2 to 5 learning together is an intentional design choice, not a compromise.
  - "Why I say 'work' instead of 'play'." A direct answer to a question prospective families genuinely have, without defensiveness.
- **Consent gate:** Typically low or none. If a classroom photo anchors the post, consent check applies.

### Pillar 5: JCC events and community moments

- **What it covers:** The fall open house, holiday programs, monthly story time, spring fundraiser, and any other JCC-affiliated events where families and prospective families connect with the school.
- **Why it matters:** Events are natural social content. They have a date, an action, and a reason to share. They also introduce prospective families to the school through a lower-stakes first encounter before committing to a tour.
- **Asset types:** Event announcement graphics (no children required), recap photos after the event (consent gate applies if children identifiable), broader community photos
- **Caption tone:** Warm and specific. What happens at this event, who it is for, and what to expect.
- **Frequency target:** Situational (roughly one post per event cycle; some months more, some months none)
- **Example post concepts:**
  - Fall open house announcement: what happens, when to arrive, what families will see.
  - Story time recap: who led it, what book, what the moment felt like in the room.
  - Spring fundraiser: what the proceeds support and how families can take part.
- **Consent gate:** Event photos of identifiable children require consent check. Crowd shots where no individual child is identifiable may be lower-risk; confirm with Rebecca before posting any event photo.

### Pillar balance

Approximate monthly mix at 2-3 posts per week:

| Pillar | Approx. frequency | Consent gate |
|---|---|---|
| Classroom life: the work | 2 of every 5 posts | Mandatory before scheduling |
| Prepared environment | 1 of every 5 posts | None (environment-only) |
| Teacher and community voices | 1 of every 5 posts | Required if children in frame |
| Montessori explained | 1 of every 5 posts | Usually none |
| JCC events and community | Situational | Required if children identifiable |

### What is NOT a pillar

- Motivational quotes or parenting wisdom graphics
- "Happy [holiday]" posts without school-specific content
- Engagement-bait prompts ("Tag a parent who needs to hear this!")
- Reposted content from national Montessori organizations without a sentence connecting it to this school specifically
- Generic language that would fit equally well on Goddard's page or any other preschool's page (Document 01)

## 4. Caption Voice and Structure

### Caption voice rules

All captions follow the Brand Voice Guide (Document 01). Social-specific additions:

- **Length:** Facebook: 50-100 words. Instagram feed: 100-250 words. Instagram Stories: no caption body; text overlay on the image.
- **Opening line:** State something specific. Not "We're so excited to share this..." or "Throwback to..." The first sentence should give the reader a reason to continue. A specific detail about what they are looking at does this more reliably than a sentiment or a feeling.
- **Paragraph breaks:** Every 2-4 sentences. No walls of text.
- **Voice:** First-person singular. Rebecca or a classroom teacher narrating directly, not an institutional voice. "I watched this happen Tuesday" is warmer than "Our teachers facilitate moments like this every day."
- **Emoji policy:** Minimal. One emoji as a closing accent is fine. Never lead with an emoji. Never stack multiple consecutive emojis.
- **CTA norm:** Most posts do not need a call to action. If one fits, it is one plain sentence. "Schedule a tour. Link in bio." is sufficient. Never pile two or three CTAs into one caption.
- **Questions in captions:** Only when genuinely asking. Never as engagement bait.
- **"We" as default:** Never. See Brand Voice Guide on first-person singular.

### Caption structure template

**Hook (first 1-2 sentences):** A specific detail. What is the reader looking at? What happened?

**Context (2-4 sentences):** The situation. What Montessori concept or classroom moment this is. Who is involved without naming identifiable children.

**Takeaway (1-2 sentences):** The single thing worth leaving with.

**CTA if applicable (1 sentence):** Clear and plain. Only if it fits.

**Hashtags:** Below the caption body, separated by a line break. On Instagram, consider the first comment for the full hashtag set. On Facebook, 2-3 hashtags inline or below.

### Examples of strong captions

**Example 1 (Pillar 1, classroom life, Instagram):**

> Tuesday morning, Threes classroom. A child set up the spooning work, sorted every last bead into the receiving tray, and returned the materials to the shelf before asking to do it again.
>
> The tray is designed so the child can check their own work without a teacher. If a bead is missing, the empty space in the tray shows it. That is control of error: not a test, just feedback built into the object.
>
> This is what I mean by "the work."

**Example 2 (Pillar 2, prepared environment, Facebook or Instagram):**

> Before anyone arrives on a Monday morning, the classroom looks like this.
>
> Every tray in place, every label readable, every material at a child's height. The prepared environment is not decoration. It is the lesson. A room that expects a child to be capable builds a child who is.

**Example 3 (Pillar 4, Montessori explained, Instagram):**

> People ask why Montessori teachers say "work" instead of "play."
>
> The short answer: work and play carry different weights, and children feel the difference. When a child is doing practical life activities (pouring, spooning, folding), they are concentrating in a way they recognize as real and purposeful. Calling it play undersells what they are doing. Calling it work respects it.
>
> It is also a lot more absorbing than most things adults call work.

**Example 4 (Pillar 3, teacher voice, Facebook):**

> Miss Carla has been in the Threes classroom for [NEEDS CONFIRMATION: years]. She can tell within the first week which materials a child is ready for, and she adjusts without making it a production.
>
> The three-period lesson is how she introduces new material: name it, ask the child to find it, ask the child to recall it. Concrete before abstract, every time.
>
> If you want to see what a Montessori classroom actually looks like in practice, come and see.

### Examples of what to avoid

- "Your children are in good hands in our nurturing environment!" (banned phrase: "nurturing environment"; Document 01)
- "Our little learners had a magical Tuesday! 🌟✨💕" (banned: "little learners," "magical"; emoji stack)
- "At Little Friends Learning Loft, we believe every child deserves a safe space to thrive and grow 🌱" (four separate violations: opener pattern, "safe space," "thrive," third-person verb about the child's future)
- "Tag a parent who would love this! 👇" (engagement bait; no substance)
- "Structured yet flexible learning for your whole child!" (banned: "structured yet flexible," "whole child"; Document 01)

### Content production workflow

The consent gate is a step in the workflow, not a separate process that happens before the workflow. No photo of an identifiable child moves forward without it.

**Step 1: Consent reconciliation**
Before any batch scheduling session, whoever owns the consent spreadsheet confirms that it is current. [NEEDS CONFIRMATION: Rebecca should assign one named person as the consent-spreadsheet owner before the first batch of scheduled posts goes out.] The no-photo list (currently 6 families) must be current and available at the time photos are selected.

**Step 2: Content selection**
Rebecca, or the designated social owner, reviews the week's Slack `#photos` posts and selects 2-3 strong candidates. Strong candidates: clear image, interesting activity visible, child identifiable (triggers consent check), or environment-only (proceeds without consent check).

**Step 3: Consent check (for any photo with identifiable children)**
Cross-reference each selected child against the current spreadsheet. If consent status is unclear or the child is on the no-photo list, the photo does not move forward. Environment-only photos skip this step.

**Step 4: Caption drafting**
Run the AI caption prompt (Section 7) for each cleared photo. Generate 3 options, select one, edit. This typically takes 10-15 minutes per post once the prompt is established.

**Step 5: Schedule**
Schedule posts in the school's scheduling tool [NEEDS CONFIRMATION: does the school currently use Later, Buffer, Meta Business Suite, or another tool? If none, Meta Business Suite is the starting recommendation as it manages both Facebook and Instagram and costs nothing.] at least 1 week ahead.

**Step 6: Day-of engagement**
15 minutes in the morning: respond to comments and DMs from the prior day. Section 8 covers protocols.

### Blackout periods

No automated posts should go out on a day when a known incident or emergency has occurred at the school or in the JCC community. Rebecca holds this judgment call. Whoever has access to the scheduling tool should know how to pause or cancel scheduled posts.

### Calendar alignment

- **Enrollment season (January through March):** Increase Pillar 4 (Montessori explained) and Pillar 3 (teacher voices) frequency. This is when prospective families are researching.
- **Event timing:** Pillar 5 posts go up 5-7 days before an event as announcement, and within 3 days after as recap.
- **Seasonal content:** Draw on the fall open house, holiday programs, and spring fundraiser as natural seasonal anchors.

### Reality check

This plan assumes approximately 2-3 hours per week of active time from whoever owns social, including consent checking, content selection, caption editing, and comment engagement. If that time is not reliably available, the cadence reduces to 2 posts per week on Facebook and 2 on Instagram, dropping the Friday post first. The Monday environment post, which requires no consent check, should be the most consistent post in the week because it has the fewest dependencies.

## 7. AI-Assisted Content Generation

### What AI can draft

- First drafts of captions for photo and carousel posts (human selects the photo and provides context; AI drafts 3 options; human edits and approves one)
- Hashtag set suggestions for specific posts (drawn from the Pillar sets in Section 5; human reviews before use)
- Caption length variations for cross-posting (same content, adjusted from Instagram length to Facebook length)
- Draft responses to simple, FAQ-level DMs (only for question types covered in Document 07; see Section 8)

### What AI must not do without human review

- Publish any post without the social account owner's explicit approval
- Generate or schedule captions for any photo featuring identifiable children before the consent check for those specific children has been completed
- Make factual claims about the school, its programs, its ratios, or its staff that are not confirmed in Document 02
- Draft responses to complaint messages, messages involving a specific child's wellbeing, or any message with emotional urgency
- Generate post copy referencing a specific enrolled child by name, even with a signed consent form

## 8. DM and Engagement Response Protocols

### DM response SLA

- **Business hours (M-F during school hours):** Same day; aim for within 4 hours
- **Evenings and weekends:** Next business morning
- **Who owns DM response:** Rebecca (primary) [NEEDS CONFIRMATION: is there a designated backup for DM coverage when Rebecca is unavailable or on vacation?]
- **Brightwheel messages from enrolled families:** Same day during care hours, per Document 10. These are handled in Brightwheel, not through Instagram or Facebook DMs.

### DM categorization

**Category 1: Enrollment or tour inquiry**
- **Action:** Respond within SLA. Move to email immediately. Do not conduct the enrollment conversation in DM.
- **Template:** "Happy to share information about our programs. The best place to do that is by email. I can send program details, available tour dates, and current enrollment status. What's the best email for you? And if you have a specific program year in mind, that's helpful to know."

**Category 2: FAQ question**
- **Action:** Answer briefly using language from Document 07 (FAQ Bank). Offer email follow-up if the parent has more questions.
- **Note:** Do not answer questions that are not covered in Document 07 from memory. If the answer is not in the FAQ Bank, respond with the offer to send a fuller answer by email.

**Category 3: Enrolled family, general message**
- **Action:** Respond warmly. If the message is about their child's day, classroom, or routine, route to Brightwheel where the classroom teacher can respond in context.

**Category 4: Vendor, partner, or JCC-related outreach**
- **Action:** Route to Rebecca. Do not commit to anything in DM.

**Category 5: Spam, solicitation, or low-quality outreach**
- **Action:** Ignore. No engagement.

**Category 6: Negative message or complaint**
- **Action:** Acknowledge within SLA. Move off-platform immediately. Do not argue in DM or in a public comment thread.
- **Template:** "I hear you, and I want to make sure this gets handled properly. My direct email is [Rebecca's email from Document 02]. Can you reach out there, or I'm happy to call you directly if that's easier."

### Comment response protocols

- **Positive comments:** Heart the comment and reply briefly within 24 hours. A specific acknowledgment is better than a generic "Thank you!"
- **Questions in comments:** Short public answer, plus "Feel free to DM me if you'd like more detail."
- **Negative comments:** One brief, professional public response at most, then move to DM or email. Do not respond multiple times publicly.
- **Photos tagged by enrolled families:** Review against the no-photo list before reposting or engaging in a way that amplifies the image. If the child is on the no-photo list, contact Rebecca before responding. If the child is consent-cleared and the parent has tagged the photo themselves, repost to Stories with credit.

### Mentions and UGC handling

- **Families tagging the school in posts:** Review against consent status before reposting. Save consent-cleared UGC to the content library (Document 04).
- **JCC or community partner tags:** Like and brief comment. Repost to Stories if the content is directly relevant to the school.
- **Any post featuring identifiable children shared or tagged from another account:** Rebecca reviews before the school engages with or amplifies it.

### Engagement initiation (outbound)

- **Follow:** The JCC, community organizations relevant to the Newburgh family audience, local businesses that enrich the school community
- **Commenting:** Natural engagement on JCC content and posts from teachers or community partners
- **Cold DM outreach:** Not a standard practice. The school does not prospect on social.

Note on targets: there is no useful number to project from if posting has been inconsistent. The right approach is to establish a 90-day consistent baseline under this playbook first, then set specific targets for the following 90 days. Inventing targets from thin data produces goals that are either meaninglessly low or serially missed.

### What is NOT the right metric

- Raw follower count alone. The school at 90% enrolled capacity does not need to reach 10,000 people. It needs to reach the right 50-100 families per year in the Newburgh draw area who are not yet in the JCC word-of-mouth circle.
- Like count on a single post that happened to spread. Spikes without conversion are noise.
- Total impressions without an engagement or click-through signal attached.
- Story views in isolation. A high story view count among already-enrolled families is nice but does not move the inquiry pipeline.

### What IS the right metric by platform role

**Facebook (primary awareness and discovery):**
- Post reach from non-followers (how many people outside the current audience are seeing the content)
- Profile visits following specific posts (consideration signal)
- DM inquiries with Facebook named as the first-touch channel

**Instagram (visual portfolio and trust reinforcement):**
- Profile visits from feed posts
- Bio link clicks (website visits from Instagram)
- Post saves (the strongest consideration signal Instagram offers; a parent who saves a post is coming back)

**Both platforms:**
- Number of tour inquiries sourced to social, captured in the inquiry form or by asking at the tour: "How did you first hear about us?"

### Quarterly optimization review

Each quarter, review the following:

1. Which posts hit the top 20% in reach or engagement: what do they share by pillar, format, or topic?
2. Which posts hit the bottom 20%: what do they share?
3. Is the consent workflow functioning smoothly? Are posts being delayed or skipped because of consent-check backlogs? If so, the bottleneck is the spreadsheet owner, not the content supply.
4. Is the cadence being hit consistently? If not, where does the workflow break down?
5. Is AI-assisted drafting producing captions that pass a brand-voice read? If the drafts need heavy editing every time, adjust the prompt.
6. Is there any meaningful shift in where local parents in Newburgh are spending time online?

### Annual playbook refresh

Full refresh when any of the following change:
- A meaningful shift in which platform the school's parent demographic uses most
- New classroom teachers (the teacher-spotlight pillar and DM routing protocols in Sections 3 and 8 are person-specific)
- A change in the consent management process that expands or restricts the usable photo pool
- The school adds a new program or meaningfully changes its offering
- The JCC relationship changes in a way that affects shared social platform use or brand presentation
