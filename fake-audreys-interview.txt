# Audrey's Farmhouse — Founder Interview
# Date: 2026-04-26
# Participants: Sally Anderson (co-founder), Doug Anderson (co-founder), Emily Michelson (Wedding Coordinator)
# Interviewer: Upriver Consulting (90 minutes, recorded with consent)
# Format: lightly cleaned transcript — kept verbal style intact

---

INTERVIEWER: Thank you both for making time. Let's start at the beginning. How did Audrey's Farmhouse come to be?

SALLY: We bought the original farmhouse in 2018. It's a c. 1740 building in Wallkill, on what used to be a commercial flower nursery. We were not in the hospitality business. Doug was an architect, I was running a small textiles business out of Brooklyn, and we were tired. The plan was to fix up the farmhouse and rent it out on weekends. The greenhouses came with the property. We didn't know what we were going to do with them.

DOUG: The greenhouses were what made us stay. We had a wedding for friends in the main greenhouse the summer after we closed. It wasn't planned — they got rained out at their original venue and we said come on up. Eighty people, dinner under the glass, candles, the whole thing. Three of those guests asked if they could book us for their own weddings before they left. That was the moment.

SALLY: We didn't even know what we were charging. Doug printed off some pricing the morning after based on what felt fair. That price held for two years.

INTERVIEWER: So this is an accidental business in some ways.

SALLY: Yes. I think that's part of why it works. We didn't come in with the wedding industry playbook. We didn't know about preferred vendor lists or floral minimums or Sunday discounts. We just thought, what would we want at our own wedding? And the answer was always: less of that, more of this.

DOUG: Less performance. More honesty.

INTERVIEWER: Talk to me about the three properties — Greenhouses, Old Mill, Friends and Neighbors. How are they different, and how are they the same?

SALLY: They share a sensibility. Nostalgic design with a modern approach is the line we use. Every property has at least one room with a working fireplace, books worth picking up, linens we'd want on our own beds. But each property has its own character.

DOUG: The Greenhouses is the big one. Three thousand square feet of glass for the main event space, the c. 1740 farmhouse, nineteen guest rooms across the property, capacity around 150 for a seated wedding. It's our flagship and it's the only property where we can host both the wedding and most of the lodging on-site.

SALLY: Old Mill is twenty-four acres, a 4,500 square foot pergola, and seven lodging units centered on a 19th century farmhouse. It's bigger and more sprawling than the Greenhouses. Different feel — more woods, more lawn, less structured. Old Mill is the property where someone might bring a dog and a group of college friends and rent the whole thing for a weekend.

DOUG: Friends and Neighbors is our newest. We bought it last year. It's in Stone Ridge, just outside the village center, on Route 209. Capacity up to 200 for events, sleeps up to 20. The building has more of a modern sensibility than the other two — cleaner lines, less of the period detail. We see it filling a gap for couples who want our service but don't want their wedding to feel like a Hudson Valley costume drama.

EMILY: Friends and Neighbors is also where we're piloting our intimate wedding packages — December through April, smaller guest counts, more flexible pricing. We launched those because we kept hearing from couples who couldn't afford a peak-season Saturday but loved the property.

INTERVIEWER: That's a great segue. Who is your customer? Who's booking these venues?

SALLY: For weddings, our core couple is between thirty and thirty-eight, household income roughly $200K to $500K, almost always from New York City or the immediate metro area, occasionally from Boston or Philly. They've usually been together five to ten years. They're not first-time event planners — they've been guests at a lot of weddings and they have opinions. They tend to be in creative or design-adjacent fields. Architects, editors, brand people, a lot of people in food.

DOUG: They are not the couple looking for a "destination wedding." They want a destination *feeling* but they want it within a two-hour drive of their apartment.

SALLY: They're people who would rather pay for a great chef than a great DJ. Who care about the quality of the linens. Who don't want a bridal suite that looks like a hotel. We've had brides ask if they can just get ready in the kitchen. The answer is yes.

EMILY: For lodging — separate from weddings — we get a lot of couples and small groups in the thirty to fifty range who are coming up from the city for a long weekend. Pet-friendly is a huge driver at Old Mill. Probably a third of our lodging bookings mention bringing a dog.

INTERVIEWER: What sends someone looking for you, specifically? What's the trigger?

SALLY: For weddings, it's almost always: they've toured a couple of "wedding factories" and felt sick. They've seen the day-of-week pricing tier sheet and the sign that says "no candles" and the dance floor that obviously gets reused, and they want out. Then a friend who knows our place tells them to look us up. We are word-of-mouth almost entirely.

DOUG: Or they've seen an Instagram post from a wedding we hosted. Photographers love our spaces. We've had photographers tell us they direct couples here.

EMILY: For lodging, the trigger is needing somewhere to bring a group — a friend's birthday weekend, an anniversary, a small reunion. They google "house rental Hudson Valley dog friendly" or they remember someone went here and call them. Airbnb fatigue is real. People want a place that's run by humans they can text.

INTERVIEWER: Tell me about the booking process. What does it look like from inquiry to wedding day?

EMILY: For weddings, an inquiry comes in through TripleSeat — we have an event inquiry link in our nav and footer. Sometimes through a direct email to me. I respond within 24 hours, almost always same day. We send a packet with pricing, sample timelines, and the list of things included versus things they bring in. Then we schedule a tour, ideally in person, sometimes virtually if they're far. After the tour, if they're interested, we send a hold letter — they can hold a date for two weeks while they think.

SALLY: Once they book, Emily becomes their main point of contact for the eight to fourteen months until the wedding. She runs everything: vendor coordination, timeline, day-of logistics. We don't do tiered "wedding packages" the way most venues do — every couple gets full coordination because we don't know how to do it any other way.

EMILY: We have a preferred vendor list — caterers, florists, photographers, DJs, officiants, hair and makeup. We don't take kickbacks. The list exists because we've worked with these people enough to know they'll show up and do good work. Couples can bring their own vendors but we steer them toward the list because of the headache cost.

INTERVIEWER: Pricing. Talk to me about pricing. Is it on the website?

DOUG: It's not on the website. That's a deliberate choice we've debated for years. The argument for putting it on the site is that we'd save Emily a lot of time on inquiries from couples who can't afford us. The argument against is that it scares away the couple in the middle who would have toured if they hadn't seen the number first.

SALLY: We are not the most expensive venue in the region and we are not the cheapest. We are squarely in the middle of premium Hudson Valley wedding pricing. Site fees range roughly $12K to $25K depending on property, season, and day of week. Catering is on top. A typical Greenhouses Saturday in peak season comes in between $80K and $130K all-in for 100-130 guests. We tell that to anyone who asks on the phone. We just don't put it on the site.

DOUG: I'd like the audit recommendation about a pricing page to be more nuanced than "you should publish your prices." We've A/B tested this. We sent half our inquirers a pricing range up front and half a tour invitation first, and the tour-first group converted at almost twice the rate. We are willing to be persuaded but it's not as simple as "publish a number."

INTERVIEWER: What did you think of the audit overall?

SALLY: The seo and the technical stuff — yes, fix it. The bit about not having a contact page that flagged as P0 — fair, we should have that. The bit about not having an FAQ page — also fair. Emily writes the same answer to the same question every week.

DOUG: The audit said our hero copy is too vague. I'm sympathetic to that but I want to push back on the alternatives. We don't want copy that sounds like every other wedding venue. "Where Hudson Valley Couples Say I Do" is fine but it's not us.

SALLY: We use words. People can tell. We don't say "magical" or "stunning" or "world-class." We say what something actually is. The greenhouse smells like the geraniums in the corner. The fireplace is a working fireplace, not a fake one. The food at Old Mill last weekend was lamb shoulder from the farm down the road. That kind of thing.

DOUG: We don't say "amazing." We don't say "perfect." We don't say "luxury." We don't say "unique" — every venue is unique in some way and the word means nothing. We don't say "magical." Magic is a word that gets used by people trying to sell you something.

INTERVIEWER: What words *should* we be using?

SALLY: Specific ones. The hero of an event isn't a feeling, it's a thing — the long table, the candles in the greenhouse, the dog asleep on the porch, the cheese board on the kitchen island. We try to write like we would write a postcard from a friend who just stayed there.

DOUG: Quiet. Generous. Considered. Lived-in. Particular.

INTERVIEWER: Let's talk about objections. What do couples push back on most?

EMILY: Three things. One — the no-pricing-on-website thing, every week. Two — capacity. Couples who want 200+ have to go to Old Mill or Friends and Neighbors. We can't do 200 at the Greenhouses and we lose those couples to Hutton Brickyards or Roxbury Barn. Three — the room block. Some couples want every guest housed on-site and our biggest property only sleeps 38. We send the rest to Hampton Inn New Paltz and they don't love that.

SALLY: A more interesting one is the seasonality push-back. The Greenhouses is glorious in spring and summer and the early fall. November through March it's still beautiful but it's smaller. The greenhouse is heated but it's not the same as June. We've been pushing the intimate wedding packages at Friends and Neighbors specifically because that property is the most weather-independent of the three.

INTERVIEWER: What questions do couples ask you that aren't on the website yet?

EMILY: Endless. "Can we bring our dog to the rehearsal dinner?" "What time do we have to be out the morning after?" "Can we have a campfire?" "Is there a generator if the power goes?" "Do you have a noise ordinance?" "How does parking work?" "Can our florist arrive at 7am?" "Where do my parents stay if we book all 19 rooms?" "Do you do brunches the next morning, included?" Half my email is the same six questions.

SALLY: We've meant to write them down for three years.

INTERVIEWER: What about for lodging guests, separately? What are they asking?

DOUG: Different questions. Pet policy specifics — fee, breed limits, where the dog can go, whether there's a yard. Group size for a buyout. What's actually in the kitchen — knives, cookware, dishwasher. What's nearby — restaurants, a hike, the closest fly-fishing river. Doctor and pharmacy in town in case someone gets sick.

INTERVIEWER: What's the brand promise? If a couple is at one of your properties on their wedding day, what do you want them to feel?

SALLY: That they hosted their wedding at a place they could imagine coming back to as a guest. That nothing about the day felt like a wedding-industrial-complex transaction. That their guests asked them, after, "where did you find this place?"

DOUG: That the day felt like theirs and not like a template. We have one wedding a weekend in season. Not three. Not a Friday-Saturday-Sunday turnover. One. The day is theirs.

SALLY: And that the people running it cared. Emily knows everyone's names by Friday night. The bartenders ask the parents about their drive up. The chef walks through the dining room during dinner to see how it's going.

INTERVIEWER: What's the one thing about your business that you want a website to communicate that the current site doesn't?

SALLY: That we're a real small business run by real people. Doug, me, Emily, our chef Michael, the team — there are sixteen of us, full-time and seasonal. Sally and Doug aren't a brand. We are people. We answer the phone.

DOUG: And that we're three different properties with three different vibes, not one venue with three pages. People show up to Old Mill expecting the Greenhouses and are confused. The site should help them self-select.

INTERVIEWER: What about the audit's recommendation around AEO and structured FAQs? You said the site doesn't have a real FAQ page, and the audit flagged that you'd lose AI-search visibility because of it.

SALLY: Yes, do that. The auditor's right. Every question Emily answers in email is a question someone is asking ChatGPT right now. Put them on the site.

EMILY: I have a Google Doc with about forty of them already typed up. I'll send it.

INTERVIEWER: One last topic — what does a successful website launch look like to you?

SALLY: Inquiries that are better qualified. Right now Emily fields inquiries from couples who clearly didn't read the site, who didn't know we have three properties, who want a beach wedding or a backyard tent. If the new site sets expectations, we should see better-fit inquiries even if the volume is similar.

DOUG: A site that doesn't embarrass us. The Squarespace site has been "fine" for four years. We don't think it's representative of the experience anymore.

EMILY: From my end — fewer of the same six questions in my inbox. If the FAQ page is good, I should be able to send a link instead of typing the same paragraph for the hundredth time.

SALLY: And one specific thing — I want guests to leave a wedding here, go home, look us up, and book a long weekend stay at Old Mill three months later. The site should do the conversion work I'm currently doing in person.

INTERVIEWER: That's helpful. We have what we need. Thank you all.

SALLY: Thank you. Send us the brief.
